🔬Research Methods

Correlation vs. Causation
  • Correlation shows a relationship (r from −1 to +1). Does NOT prove causation.
  • Only experiments can establish causation.
  • Independent variable (IV): the variable manipulated.
  • Dependent variable (DV): the variable measured.
  • Control group: no manipulation. Experimental group: receives manipulation.
  • Double-blind: neither participants nor researchers know group assignment.
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Which research method is the ONLY one that can establish cause-and-effect?
  • A) Case study
  • B) Survey
  • C) Experiment
  • D) Naturalistic observation
C: Experiment. Only experiments manipulate an independent variable and control other factors, allowing causal conclusions. Correlational methods identify relationships but cannot rule out third variables or directionality.
2. Negative reinforcement is best defined as:
  • A) Punishment that decreases behavior
  • B) Removing an aversive stimulus to increase behavior
  • C) Adding a pleasant stimulus to increase behavior
  • D) Ignoring a behavior to decrease it
B is correct. CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Negative reinforcement INCREASES behavior by REMOVING something unpleasant. It is NOT punishment. Example: taking Tylenol removes a headache (aversive stimulus), so you're more likely to take Tylenol again.

🧠Brain Regions

Limbic System

Amygdala = fear/aggression/emotion. Hippocampus = NEW memory formation (not storage). Hypothalamus = hunger, thirst, body temperature, fight-or-flight trigger.

Cerebral Cortex Lobes

Frontal = planning, judgment, personality, motor strip. Parietal = touch, spatial. Occipital = vision. Temporal = hearing, language (Wernicke's area).

Brainstem

Medulla = breathing, heartbeat (survival). Pons = sleep, arousal. Cerebellum = balance, coordination, procedural memory.

Language Areas

Broca's area (frontal lobe) = SPEECH PRODUCTION. Damage → Broca's aphasia (halting speech). Wernicke's area (temporal lobe) = language COMPREHENSION. Damage → Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless).

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. The hippocampus is most associated with:
  • A) Fear and aggression
  • B) Formation of new long-term memories
  • C) Language production
  • D) Motor coordination
B is correct. The hippocampus converts short-term memories into long-term ones. Patient H.M. had his hippocampus removed and developed anterograde amnesia — he could not form new explicit memories. His old memories were intact (stored elsewhere in the cortex).
2. The 'fight-or-flight' response is controlled by the:
  • A) Parasympathetic nervous system
  • B) Somatic nervous system
  • C) Sympathetic nervous system
  • D) Central nervous system
C: Sympathetic nervous system. Sympathetic = fight-or-flight: increases heart rate, dilates pupils, inhibits digestion, releases adrenaline. Parasympathetic = rest-and-digest: reverses all these. An easy mnemonic: Sympathetic = 'Stress response.'

🔄Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

Classical Conditioning Terms
  • UCS (Unconditioned Stimulus): naturally causes response (food → salivation).
  • UCR (Unconditioned Response): natural response to UCS (salivation).
  • CS (Conditioned Stimulus): neutral stimulus paired with UCS (bell).
  • CR (Conditioned Response): learned response to CS alone (salivation to bell).
  • Extinction: CR weakens when CS presented without UCS repeatedly.
  • Spontaneous Recovery: Extinguished CR reappears after rest period.
  • Generalization: Responding to stimuli similar to the CS.
  • Discrimination: Responding only to specific CS, not similar stimuli.

Reinforcement Schedules
  • Fixed Ratio (FR): After set number of responses. High, steady rate. (Every 10 items = bonus.)
  • Variable Ratio (VR): Unpredictable number of responses. HIGHEST rate, MOST resistant to extinction. (Slot machines.)
  • Fixed Interval (FI): After set time. Scallop pattern — slow then burst. (Weekly quiz.)
  • Interval (VI): Unpredictable time. Steady, slow rate. (Checking email randomly.)
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. A student cleans their room to stop their parent's nagging. This is an example of:
  • A) Positive reinforcement
  • B) Negative reinforcement
  • C) Positive punishment
  • D) Negative punishment
B: Negative reinforcement. An aversive stimulus (nagging) is REMOVED when the behavior occurs (cleaning), making the behavior more likely in the future. Negative = remove. Reinforcement = increase behavior.
2. Which reinforcement schedule produces behavior most resistant to extinction?
  • A) Fixed ratio
  • B) Fixed interval
  • C) Variable ratio
  • D) Variable interval
C: Variable ratio. Because reinforcement is unpredictable, the organism keeps responding hoping the next response will be reinforced — just like gambling. This is why slot machines are so addictive.

Fundamental Attribution Error

Overestimating personality/disposition and underestimating situational factors when explaining OTHERS' behavior. We explain our own with situation.

Cognitive Dissonance

Discomfort when beliefs and behavior conflict. We change beliefs to match behavior (post-purchase rationalization).

Social Facilitation

Presence of others improves performance on simple/well-learned tasks but hurts performance on difficult/novel tasks.

Groupthink

Group prioritizes harmony over critical thinking, leading to poor decisions. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, self-censorship.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. The Fundamental Attribution Error states that we tend to:
  • A) Blame ourselves for others' failures
  • B) Overestimate situational factors when explaining others' behavior
  • C) Overestimate dispositional factors and underestimate situational factors when explaining OTHERS' behavior
  • D) Attribute our own failures to personality flaws
C is correct. When we see someone trip, we think 'clumsy person' (disposition) rather than 'uneven sidewalk' (situation). For our own behavior, we do the opposite — we blame the situation. This is the actor-observer bias.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Schizophrenia is most associated with excess activity of which neurotransmitter?
  • A) Serotonin
  • B) GABA
  • C) Dopamine
  • D) Acetylcholine
C: Dopamine. The dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia holds that excess dopamine activity (especially in mesolimbic pathways) causes positive symptoms. Antipsychotic medications work by blocking dopamine D2 receptors.
2. Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) primarily works by:
  • A) Prescribing medication to correct neurotransmitter imbalances
  • B) Identifying and challenging distorted thought patterns that lead to maladaptive behaviors
  • C) Exploring unconscious conflicts from childhood
  • D) Using classical conditioning to replace negative responses
B is correct. CBT is based on the cognitive model: distorted thoughts (cognitive distortions like catastrophizing and all-or-nothing thinking) drive emotional distress and maladaptive behavior. By identifying and reframing these thoughts, symptoms improve.
Perfect Score Checklist
  • Negative reinforcement INCREASES behavior by REMOVING an aversive stimulus — NOT punishment
  • Hippocampus = new memory FORMATION; amygdala = fear/emotion
  • Variable ratio schedule = most resistant to extinction (slot machines)
  • Dopamine low → Parkinson's; excess → schizophrenia. Serotonin low → depression.
  • Fundamental Attribution Error: overestimate disposition, underestimate situation for OTHERS
  • Milgram: 65% maximum shock — authority overrides morality
  • Bystander effect: more witnesses = less helping (diffusion of responsibility)
  • CBT = most evidence-based treatment for most anxiety and mood disorders
  • Classical conditioning: UCS → UCR; CS+UCS → paired; CS alone → CR
  • Broca's = speech production; Wernicke's = language comprehension
