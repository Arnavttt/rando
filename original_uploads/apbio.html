🧬Cell Membranes & Transport

Fluid Mosaic Model & Transport
  • Phospholipid bilayer: Hydrophilic heads face water; hydrophobic tails face inward.
  • Passive transport: With gradient, no energy. Simple diffusion (nonpolar), facilitated (protein channels).
  • Active transport: Against gradient, requires ATP. Na⁺/K⁺ pump (3 Na out, 2 K in).
  • Osmosis: Water moves toward higher solute (lower water potential). Hypertonic = water leaves. Hypotonic = water enters.
  • Endocytosis/Exocytosis: Bulk transport via vesicles.

  • B) Remain unchanged

  • B) Proteins

C: Lipids (~9 kcal/g vs. ~4 for carbs/proteins). Fats are more reduced (more C-H bonds to oxidize), making them more energy-dense.

Checkpoints & Cancer
  • G1 checkpoint: Is the cell large enough? Is DNA undamaged? Controlled by cyclins/CDKs.
  • G2 checkpoint: Was DNA replicated correctly? Checks for damage before mitosis.
  • M checkpoint (spindle): Are all chromosomes attached to spindle fibers?
  • Cancer = checkpoint failure. Proto-oncogenes (accelerators) mutate to oncogenes → uncontrolled division. Tumor suppressor genes (brakes) like p53 mutated → checkpoints lost.
  • Telomeres shorten with each division (Hayflick limit). Cancer cells express telomerase — restoring telomeres = cellular immortality.

🧬Meiosis I — Reductive Division

Meiosis I — Step by Step
1
Prophase I (most complex): Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) forming tetrads (bivalents). Crossing over occurs at chiasmata — arms of homologs exchange DNA segments. This creates genetic recombination. (This is the KEY event that doesn't happen in mitosis.)
2
Metaphase I: Tetrads align at the metaphase plate. UNLIKE mitosis — homolog pairs align, not individual chromosomes. Independent assortment occurs here: which side each homolog goes to is random. With 23 chromosome pairs → 2²³ ≈ 8 million possible arrangements.
3
Anaphase I: Homologs (not sister chromatids!) pulled to opposite poles. Sister chromatids remain attached at centromere. Cell goes from 2n → each pole has n chromosomes (but each is still a pair of sister chromatids).
4
Telophase I + Cytokinesis: Two haploid cells formed. No DNA replication occurs between Meiosis I and II.

🧬Meiosis II — Equatorial Division

Meiosis II — Step by Step
1
Prophase II: Spindle forms in each of the two haploid cells. No DNA replication!
2
Metaphase II: Individual chromosomes (each still 2 sister chromatids) align at metaphase plate — now like mitosis.
3
Anaphase II: Sister chromatids finally separate — pulled to opposite poles.
4
Telophase II + Cytokinesis: Four haploid cells produced. Each has 23 chromosomes, each a single chromatid. In males: 4 sperm (spermatogenesis). In females: 1 egg + 3 polar bodies (oogenesis).

🧬Sources of Genetic Variation in Meiosis

1. Crossing Over (Recombination)

During Prophase I, homologs exchange DNA segments at chiasmata. Creates new allele combinations on a single chromosome. Most powerful source of variation — can create virtually unlimited combinations.

2. Independent Assortment

During Metaphase I, which homolog of each pair goes to which pole is random. 2²³ ≈ 8 million possible chromosome combinations in humans (per gamete). Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment reflects this.

3. Random Fertilization

Any sperm (8 million+ combinations) can fertilize any egg (8 million+ combinations) → 64 trillion+ possible genotypes per couple.

4. Nondisjunction

Failure of chromosomes to separate properly. In Meiosis I: homologs don't separate. In Meiosis II: sister chromatids don't separate. Results: aneuploidy. Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) = extra chromosome 21. Monosomy X (Turner syndrome) = 45, X.

Common Exam Traps — Mitosis vs. Meiosis
  • Crossing over occurs in Prophase I of MEIOSIS, not mitosis.
  • Homologs pair during Meiosis I only — not mitosis, not Meiosis II.
  • After Meiosis I, cells are haploid but chromosomes still have TWO sister chromatids each — they split in Meiosis II.
  • Nondisjunction in Meiosis I creates MORE severe aneuploidy (affects more gametes) than in Meiosis II.
  • Spermatogenesis produces 4 functional sperm. Oogenesis produces 1 egg + 3 polar bodies (unequal cytokinesis conserves cytoplasm for the egg).
  • Mitosis produces cells with identical DNA to parent. Meiosis produces cells that are never identical due to recombination + assortment.
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Crossing over in meiosis occurs between:
  • A) Sister chromatids of the same chromosome
  • B) Homologous chromosomes during Prophase I
  • C) Any two chromosomes during Anaphase
  • D) Sister chromatids during Meiosis II
B: Homologous chromosomes during Prophase I. Crossing over (recombination) occurs when non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange corresponding DNA segments at chiasmata during Prophase I. This is genetically significant because it creates new combinations of alleles that didn't exist on either parent chromosome.
2. A cell undergoes nondisjunction during Meiosis I. How many of the resulting gametes will have an abnormal chromosome number?
  • A) 1 out of 4
  • B) 2 out of 4
  • C) All 4
  • D) 0 out of 4
C: All 4. Nondisjunction in Meiosis I means homologs fail to separate. Both homologs end up in the same cell. After Meiosis II, ALL 4 resulting gametes are abnormal — 2 will have an extra chromosome (n+1) and 2 will be missing one (n-1). Compare: nondisjunction in Meiosis II only affects 2 of the 4 gametes.
3. Which event in meiosis directly explains Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment?
  • A) Crossing over during Prophase I
  • B) Separation of sister chromatids in Anaphase II
  • C) Random alignment of homologous pairs at Metaphase I
  • D) Synapsis forming bivalents
C is correct. Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment states that alleles of different genes are distributed independently. This occurs because during Metaphase I, each homologous pair lines up at the metaphase plate independently — the orientation of one pair doesn't affect any other pair. This produces 2²³ possible gamete types in humans from chromosome assortment alone.
4. During oogenesis (egg formation), meiosis produces:
  • A) 4 equally-sized egg cells
  • B) 1 large secondary oocyte and 3 polar bodies
  • C) 2 haploid cells, each dividing once more
  • D) 1 egg and 1 polar body
B: 1 egg + 3 polar bodies. Oogenesis uses unequal cytokinesis to concentrate cytoplasm and nutrients into one cell. Primary oocyte → (Meiosis I) secondary oocyte + 1st polar body. Secondary oocyte → (Meiosis II) egg + 2nd polar body. The 1st polar body may also divide, giving 3 total polar bodies. All polar bodies degenerate.

🧬Photosynthesis ATP Accounting

Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Light Reactions (thylakoid membrane): Capture light → ATP + NADPH + O₂. Water splits. PS II → ETC → PS I → NADP⁺ reductase.
  • Calvin Cycle (stroma): ATP + NADPH + CO₂ → G3P. 3 turns net 1 G3P. Carbon fixation by RuBisCO.
  • C3 plants: Normal Calvin Cycle. CO₂ → 3-carbon compound directly.
  • C4 plants: CO₂ fixed into 4-carbon compound in mesophyll → shuttled to bundle sheath → Calvin Cycle. Reduces photorespiration (corn, sugarcane).
  • CAM plants: Open stomata at night, store CO₂ as malate, do Calvin Cycle by day (cacti, pineapple).

🧬Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Hardy-Weinberg: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 and p + q = 1
  • p = dominant allele freq; q = recessive allele freq
  • p² = homozygous dominant; 2pq = heterozygous (carriers); q² = homozygous recessive (shows trait)
  • 5 HW conditions: Large population, random mating, no mutation, no gene flow (migration), no natural selection.
  • Exam shortcut: Always find q from q² first → then p = 1−q → then 2pq for carrier freq.

Incomplete Dominance

Heterozygote is intermediate: red × white = pink. Alleles unchanged — only phenotype appears blended.

Codominance

Both alleles expressed simultaneously: ABO blood type AB shows both A and B antigens.

Sex-Linked

X-linked recessive expressed more in males (only one X, no second copy to mask). Hemophilia, color blindness.

Epistasis

One gene masks the expression of another. ABO blood type masked by Bombay phenotype (h gene).

🧬Gene Expression

Central Dogma + Regulation
  • Transcription: DNA → mRNA (nucleus). RNA polymerase reads 3'→5', synthesizes 5'→3'.
  • Translation: mRNA → protein (ribosome). tRNA anticodons match codons. AUG = start. UAA/UAG/UGA = stop.
  • lac operon: ON when lactose present (allolactose removes repressor). Negative control.
  • trp operon: ON when tryptophan absent (tryptophan activates repressor = off). Negative control.
  • Eukaryotic regulation: Enhancers, silencers, transcription factors. Methylation = off. Histone acetylation = on.

  • A) 9%
  • B) 30%
  • C) 42%
  • D) 48%
C: 42%. q² = 0.09 → q = 0.3 → p = 0.7 → 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42.
2. The lac operon is ON when:
  • A) Lactose is absent
  • B) Glucose is present
  • C) Lactose is present (allolactose blocks the repressor)
  • D) tRNA binds the operator
C is correct. Lactose → allolactose → binds lac repressor → repressor falls off operator → RNA polymerase transcribes lac genes. Without lactose, repressor blocks transcription.

🧬Mechanisms of Evolution

Natural Selection

Heritable variation + differential reproductive success. Directional (one extreme), stabilizing (intermediate), disruptive (both extremes).

Genetic Drift

Random allele frequency changes. Bottleneck effect (population crash) and founder effect (small colonizing group) are forms. Greater in small populations.

Gene Flow

Movement of alleles between populations via migration. Tends to reduce genetic differences between populations.

Speciation

Allopatric: geographic isolation. Sympatric: polyploidy in plants. Prezygotic vs. postzygotic reproductive barriers.

🧬Ecology — Population Growth

Exponential: dN/dt = rN Logistic: dN/dt = rN × (K−N)/K // max rate when N = K/2 10% Energy Rule: Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary → Tertiary 100% 10% 1% 0.1% Trophic level efficiency = (energy at level n+1 / energy at level n) × 100%
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Genetic drift would have the greatest effect on allele frequencies in:
  • A) A large, isolated population
  • B) A small population that just survived a disaster (bottleneck)
  • C) A population with high migration
  • D) A population under strong directional selection
B is correct. Genetic drift is random and has disproportionately large effects in small populations. A bottleneck event drastically reduces population size, and random chance — not fitness — determines which alleles survive. Alleles can go to fixation or be lost entirely through drift alone.
2. Logistic population growth rate is maximized when N equals:
  • A) 0
  • B) K/4
  • C) K/2
  • D) K
C: K/2. dN/dt = rN(K−N)/K. This product is maximized at N=K/2 — large enough population for high birth rate, but enough resources remaining to sustain fast growth.
