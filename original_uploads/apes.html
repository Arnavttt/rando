ConceptDetails
10% RuleOnly ~10% of energy transfers between trophic levels. 90% lost as heat, movement, waste.
Trophic LevelsProducers → Primary consumers → Secondary → Tertiary. Each level 10× less energy.
Gross vs. Net ProductivityGPP = total photosynthesis. NPP = GPP minus plant respiration. NPP available to consumers.

🌿Biogeochemical Cycles

Carbon Cycle

Carbon in atmosphere (CO₂) → photosynthesis → organic matter → respiration/decomposition → back to atmosphere. FAST cycle: days–years. SLOW cycle: fossil fuel formation (millions of years). Combustion of fossil fuels = excess CO₂ → greenhouse effect.

Nitrogen Cycle

N₂ (unusable) → nitrogen fixation (bacteria, lightning) → NH₃ → nitrification → NO₃⁻ (usable) → plant uptake → denitrification → N₂. Human disruption: synthetic fertilizers (Haber-Bosch), runoff → eutrophication.

Phosphorus Cycle

No atmospheric component. Weathering releases phosphate from rock → soil/water → plants/animals → decomposition. Runoff from fertilizers = eutrophication. Mined as fertilizer — non-renewable on human timescale.

Water Cycle

Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration, groundwater. Deforestation reduces transpiration and increases surface runoff.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. A lake receives excess phosphorus and nitrogen from agricultural runoff. The MOST LIKELY consequence is:
  • A) Increased fish populations
  • B) Eutrophication — algae blooms deplete oxygen → hypoxic dead zones
  • C) Higher water clarity
  • D) Decreased plant growth
B: Eutrophication. Excess nutrients → algae bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them → bacteria use all O₂ → fish die (hypoxic/anoxic zone). The Gulf of Mexico dead zone is driven by Mississippi River agricultural runoff.
2. The Haber-Bosch process most directly affects which biogeochemical cycle?
  • A) Carbon cycle
  • B) Phosphorus cycle
  • C) Nitrogen cycle
  • D) Water cycle
C: Nitrogen cycle. Haber-Bosch industrially fixes atmospheric N₂ into NH₃ (ammonia) for synthetic fertilizers. This has dramatically increased food production but also caused massive disruption of the nitrogen cycle through runoff, eutrophication, and N₂O (nitrous oxide) — a potent greenhouse gas.

🌿Population Ecology — Key Equations

Rule of 70 (doubling time): Doubling time (years) = 70 / annual growth rate (%) Example: 2% growth rate → 70/2 = 35 years to double Population growth rate: % growth rate = [(birth rate − death rate) / 1000] × 100 (or add immigration / subtract emigration for total change) Total Fertility Rate (TFR): TFR < 2.1 → population eventually DECREASES (below replacement) TFR = 2.1 → stable population TFR > 2.1 → population GROWS

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. A country has a birth rate of 35/1000 and a death rate of 10/1000. Its annual growth rate is:
  • A) 3.5%
  • B) 2.5%
  • C) 10%
  • D) 0.025%
B: 2.5%. (Birth rate − Death rate)/1000 × 100 = (35−10)/1000 × 100 = 25/1000 × 100 = 2.5%. Doubling time = 70/2.5 = 28 years.
2. A country in Stage 2 of the DTM is characterized by:
  • A) Low birth and death rates
  • B) High birth rates and rapidly falling death rates → rapid population growth
  • C) Below-replacement birth rates
  • D) Both high birth and death rates
B is correct. Stage 2 begins when death rates decline (due to improved sanitation, medicine, food supply) while birth rates remain high — creating rapid natural population increase.

🌿Biodiversity Threats — HIPPCO

HIPPCO — Major Causes of Biodiversity Loss (in order of impact)
  • H — Habitat destruction (by far the #1 cause — deforestation, wetland draining, urban sprawl)
  • I — Invasive species (displace natives; no natural predators; e.g., zebra mussels, kudzu, brown tree snake)
  • P — Population growth (driving all other pressures through resource demand)
  • P — Pollution (water, air, soil; includes light and noise pollution; endocrine disruptors)
  • C — Climate change (shifting ranges; phenology mismatches; coral bleaching; sea level rise)
  • O — Overexploitation (overfishing, poaching, bushmeat hunting)

🌿Pollution — Key Distinctions

Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants

Primary: emitted directly (CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates from combustion). Secondary: formed from reactions in atmosphere (ozone O₃, NO₂, photochemical smog, acid rain H₂SO₄).

Climate Change vs. Ozone Depletion

Different problems, different causes! Climate: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, water vapor trap heat (greenhouse effect). Ozone: CFCs destroy stratospheric O₃ → UV exposure. Fix: Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFCs. Climate: Paris Agreement (2015) — nonbinding emissions targets.

Point vs. Nonpoint Pollution

Point: identifiable single source (factory pipe, sewage outfall — regulated under Clean Water Act). Nonpoint: diffuse sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater) — much harder to regulate.

Eutrophication

Excess N or P → algae bloom → decomposers consume O₂ → hypoxic zone. Prevented by: buffer strips, wetlands, reduced fertilizer use, tertiary wastewater treatment.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. The Montreal Protocol (1987) addressed which environmental problem?
  • A) Acid rain
  • B) Climate change
  • C) Stratospheric ozone depletion by CFCs
  • D) Eutrophication
C: Ozone depletion. CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) catalytically destroy stratospheric ozone (O₃). The Montreal Protocol banned CFCs — it is considered the most successful international environmental treaty. The ozone layer is slowly recovering. Do NOT confuse with the Paris Agreement (2015, climate change).
2. A farmer applies excess nitrogen fertilizer. Rank the most likely sequence of events:
  • A) Runoff → eutrophication → algae bloom → hypoxia → fish kills
  • B) Runoff → fish kills → algae bloom → ozone depletion
  • C) Algae bloom → acid rain → hypoxia
  • D) Volatilization → greenhouse effect → drought
A is correct. Classic eutrophication sequence: excess nutrients → algae bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them → bacteria consume dissolved O₂ → hypoxic conditions → fish and other aquatic life die. The Gulf Dead Zone is the signature APES example.

🌿Climate Science Fundamentals

Greenhouse Effect & Climate Change
  • Greenhouse gases: CO₂ (fossil fuels, deforestation), CH₄ (livestock, rice, landfills, natural gas), N₂O (fertilizers, combustion), H₂O, CFCs.
  • Albedo feedback: Melting ice → less reflective surface → more heat absorbed → more warming.
  • Ocean acidification: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → lower ocean pH → dissolves shells (calcium carbonate).
  • Sea level rise causes: (1) thermal expansion of water, (2) melting glaciers/ice sheets.
  • IPCC: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change — synthesizes climate science. Reports used for policy.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Ocean acidification is MOST directly caused by:
  • A) Acid rain falling into the ocean
  • B) Ocean absorption of CO₂ from the atmosphere forming carbonic acid
  • C) Industrial wastewater discharge
  • D) Stratospheric ozone depletion
B is correct. Oceans absorb ~30% of CO₂ emissions. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ → lowers pH. Since industrialization, ocean pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1 — a 25% increase in acidity (pH is logarithmic). This threatens shell-forming organisms (oysters, corals, pteropods).
