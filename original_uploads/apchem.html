IMF Strength (Weakest → Strongest)
  • London Dispersion: All molecules. Strength ∝ molar mass and polarizability.
  • Dipole-Dipole: Polar molecules only.
  • Hydrogen Bonding: H bonded to N, O, or F. Explains water's high bp.
  • Ion-Dipole: Ion near polar molecule — strongest IMF.
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. Which molecule has a bent geometry despite having 4 electron groups?
  • A) CH₄
  • B) NH₃
  • C) H₂O
  • D) CO₂
C: H₂O. H₂O has 4 electron groups (2 bonding + 2 lone pairs) → tetrahedral electron geometry but BENT molecular geometry. The lone pairs repel more strongly, compressing the bond angle to ~104.5°.

⚗️Kinetics — Rate Laws & Integrated Rate Laws

Rate Law: rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ // orders from EXPERIMENT, never coefficients Integrated Rate Laws: Zero order: [A] = [A]₀ − kt // linear: [A] vs t First order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ − kt // linear: ln[A] vs t Second order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt // linear: 1/[A] vs t Half-life: First order: t½ = 0.693/k // CONSTANT — independent of [A]₀ Second order: t½ = 1/(k[A]₀) // depends on initial concentration Arrhenius: k = Ae^(−Ea/RT) // catalyst lowers Ea, increases rate

⚗️Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle
  • Add reactant / remove product: shifts RIGHT (toward products)
  • Add product / remove reactant: shifts LEFT
  • Increase pressure: shifts toward fewer moles of gas
  • Increase temperature: shifts toward endothermic side (treats heat as reactant/product)
  • Add catalyst: equilibrium UNCHANGED — just reaches it faster. K does not change.
  • Add inert gas at constant volume: NO shift — partial pressures unchanged
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. For N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (ΔH=−92 kJ), which increases NH₃ yield?
  • A) Increasing temperature
  • B) Increasing pressure
  • C) Adding a catalyst
  • D) Decreasing pressure
B: Increasing pressure. 4 moles gas → 2 moles. Increasing pressure favors fewer gas moles → shifts right → more NH₃. Choice A would shift LEFT (exothermic reaction, adding heat shifts away from products). Catalyst (C) doesn't change yield.
2. Reaction orders are determined by:
  • A) Balanced equation stoichiometric coefficients
  • B) Experiment only
  • C) The rate-determining step only
  • D) Activation energy
B: Experiment only. This is one of the most common exam misconceptions. Stoichiometric coefficients in the balanced equation NEVER directly give rate law orders (except in elementary reactions where the mechanism IS the stoichiometry).

⚗️pH, Ka, and Henderson-Hasselbalch

pH = −log[H⁺] // pOH = −log[OH⁻] // pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C Ka × Kb = Kw = 1×10⁻¹⁴ // at 25°C Stronger acid = smaller Ka = smaller pKa Weak acid ICE table: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻ I: C 0 0 C: −x +x +x E: C−x x x Ka = x²/(C−x) ≈ x²/C // 5% approximation valid when x << C [H⁺] = x = √(Ka·C) Henderson-Hasselbalch (buffer): pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) // Buffer most effective when pH ≈ pKa
📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. At the half-equivalence point in a weak acid titration:
  • A) pH = 7
  • B) pH = pKa
  • C) pH = pKb
  • D) pH = 14
B: pH = pKa. At half-equivalence, half the acid has been converted to its conjugate base: [HA] = [A⁻]. Substituting into H-H: pH = pKa + log(1) = pKa + 0 = pKa. This is used to FIND Ka experimentally from a titration curve.
Perfect Score Checklist
  • Reaction orders from EXPERIMENT — never stoichiometric coefficients
  • First-order half-life is CONSTANT: t½ = 0.693/k
  • Catalyst: lowers Ea, increases rate, does NOT change K or ΔG°
  • Le Chatelier: adding inert gas at constant volume = NO shift
  • pH = pKa at half-equivalence point
  • Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
  • Stronger acid = smaller Ka = more dissociation
  • H bonding requires H bonded to N, O, or F
  • VSEPR: lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs
  • Anode = oxidation (always); cathode = reduction (always)
