2025 Analytic Rubric — All 3 FRQs
6 Points Total

📋The 6-Point Analytic Rubric (applies to FRQs 1, 2, and 3)

Row A — Thesis / Claim0–1 points
1 pt
Responds to the prompt with a defensible thesis that establishes a line of reasoning.

• For Argument and Synthesis: makes a CLAIM about the issue that can be supported.
• For Rhetorical Analysis: makes a claim about HOW rhetorical choices achieve the writer's purpose.
• Can appear anywhere in the essay (intro or conclusion).
• Must be MORE than a restatement of the prompt. Must be MORE than a statement of fact.
0 pts
Restates or paraphrases the prompt. States a fact rather than making a claim. Offers only a plan to argue rather than an argument itself.
Row B — Evidence and Commentary0–4 points
0 pts
Simply restates thesis, summarizes the prompt, or offers evidence irrelevant to the argument.
1 pt
Provides evidence but that evidence is general rather than specific; commentary is absent or merely summarizes.
2 pts
Provides some specific evidence; commentary explains how some evidence relates to the argument (but may be superficial or inconsistent).
3 pts
Specific evidence consistently supporting the argument; commentary that explains HOW the evidence supports the line of reasoning.

• Synthesis: cites at least 3 sources; commentary connects each to the argument.
• Rhetorical Analysis: identifies specific choices; explains effect on audience.
• Argument: specific examples with commentary linking to claim.
4 pts
All of the above plus: commentary consistently explains how multiple pieces of evidence work together to support an overall line of reasoning.

• Synthesis: sources are integrated and analyzed, not just cited.
• Rhetorical Analysis: explains how MULTIPLE choices work together to achieve the purpose.
• Argument: demonstrates understanding of the full complexity of the issue.

Note: grammatical errors that interfere with communication prevent the 4th point.
Row C — Sophistication0–1 points
1 pt
Demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the rhetorical situation, issue, or text through one of these pathways:

• Nuanced argument: explicitly acknowledges and explores complexities, tensions, or contradictions within the issue.
• Broader context: situates the argument in a larger context (historical, cultural, political, ethical).
• Vivid persuasive style: consistently compelling voice and skillful use of language throughout — not one strong sentence.

This is earned by roughly 5–15% of essays. It cannot be earned with a throwaway 'some might argue...' sentence. It must be evident THROUGHOUT.
0 pts
Demonstrates a simplistic or formulaic understanding. Complexity is asserted but not demonstrated.
How to Think About the Rubric
  • Thesis (Row A): The entrance ticket. No thesis = max score of 3 regardless of how good the rest is.
  • Commentary (Row B): The engine. Most students stall at a 2 because they identify devices or cite sources without explaining the 'so what?' — how does this specific evidence prove this specific claim?
  • Sophistication (Row C): The bonus. Don't chase it — write a genuinely complex argument and it may appear. A formulaic 'on the other hand' never earns it.
  • Score target: A 5-scoring essay typically earns 1 + 3-4 + 0 = 4–5 points. A 4 + 1 = 6 is exceptional writing.

🎯The Four Rhetorical Appeals — Including Kairos

Ethos — Credibility

Appeal to the speaker's character, expertise, and trustworthiness. Built through: credentials, shared values, fair treatment of the opposition, personal experience, professional tone. Undermined by: logical fallacies, misrepresentation, dismissiveness.

Pathos — Emotion

Appeal to the audience's emotions, values, and imagination. Built through: anecdotes, vivid imagery, word choice with strong connotation, direct address, appeals to family/community/identity. Risk: excessive pathos without logos = manipulation, which can undermine ethos.

Logos — Logic

Appeal to reason, evidence, and argument. Built through: statistics, expert testimony, cause-effect reasoning, logical structure, precedent. Risk: logos alone can feel cold or abstract — often most effective when combined with pathos.

Kairos — The Right Moment

The appeal to timing and context. A kairos argument exploits a unique window of opportunity — the argument is compelling specifically because of when and where it is being made. Kairos answers the question: why does this argument need to be made right now?

Kairos — Deep Dive
  • Definition: From ancient Greek — the 'opportune moment' or 'right time.' A kairotic argument derives its force from its context. Remove it from that moment, and it loses much of its power.
  • Classic examples in AP texts:
  • • MLK's 'Letter from Birmingham Jail': written from jail, as a response to specific clergymen's criticism — the exigence is everything. The timing, the location (jail), the audience (moderate white clergy) make every argument kairotic.
  • • Lincoln's Second Inaugural: delivered as the Civil War neared its end. The call for 'malice toward none' only works BECAUSE the war is nearly won and the nation must choose how to reunite.
  • • A CEO's apology after a scandal: its rhetorical effectiveness depends entirely on whether it comes before or after evidence is public.
  • How to write about kairos: 'By addressing this argument in the immediate aftermath of [event], [speaker] exploits a moment when the audience is emotionally primed to reconsider [belief] — a window that would have closed within weeks.'
  • Kairos earns the sophistication point when used to explain WHY a specific rhetorical choice was not only effective but timed to maximize persuasion.

📝 Unit Quiz 0/0 correct
1. A speaker opens an argument about immigration policy by noting, 'I stand before you today as the child of immigrants.' Which appeal is primarily at work?
  • A) Logos
  • B) Pathos
  • C) Kairos
  • D) Ethos
D: Ethos. The speaker establishes personal credibility — lived experience as an immigrant's child gives them a form of ethos (authority from experience) on this specific topic. However, a strong essay would also note the pathos element (the audience may feel sympathy or connection) and ask HOW this claim positions the audience to receive the argument.
2. Which of the following best describes Kairos?
  • A) An appeal to the audience's emotions through vivid narrative
  • B) A logical argument based on statistical evidence
  • C) An argument whose persuasive force derives from the specific timing and context of its delivery
  • D) The speaker's established credibility based on credentials
C is correct. Kairos is the appeal of timing — the argument is powerful specifically because of when and where it is being made. A eulogy is kairotic: the same words at a dinner party would be inappropriate and ineffective. Analyzing kairos in AP Lang means asking: what about this specific moment makes this argument uniquely compelling, urgent, or necessary?
3. A writer concludes an essay with: 'It is not too late to act. It is not too late to choose. It is not too late to lead.' This is an example of:
  • A) Asyndeton
  • B) Chiasmus
  • C) Anaphora with tricolon
  • D) Epistrophe
C: Anaphora with tricolon. 'It is not too late' repeated at the beginning = anaphora. Three parallel clauses = tricolon. The combination builds urgency and momentum, with each verb (act, choose, lead) escalating from individual to civic to moral responsibility. This is also a kairotic move — the word 'still' implies a closing window of opportunity.
FRQ 1
Synthesis Essay — Complete Structure
~40 min · ~18%

✍️What the Synthesis Essay Is Actually Asking

The Task
  • You are given 6–7 sources (texts, images, graphs, data sets). You have 15 minutes to read them.
  • Your job: develop your own position on the issue and support it using evidence from at least 3 sources.
  • You are NOT summarizing what each source says. You are making an argument and the sources are evidence.
  • The sources represent multiple perspectives — your thesis must take a position, not just describe the debate.

📐Complete Essay Structure — Synthesis

SYNTHESIS ESSAY — FULL STRUCTURE
1
Introduction (3–5 sentences)
Sentence 1–2: Brief, specific context for the issue — what is the debate, and why does it matter? (Not a broad generalization like "Throughout history...")
Sentence 3–4: THESIS — your defensible claim. Use the Heimler formula:
"Although [strongest counterpoint], [your specific position], because [reason 1] and [reason 2]."
Do not say "I will argue that..." or "There are many factors..." Say what you actually believe and why.
2
Body Paragraph 1 — First Line of Reasoning (8–12 sentences)
Topic sentence: First reason your position is correct (one specific claim).
Evidence from Source: Introduce the source, give specific data/quote/example. Cite: (Source A) or descriptive reference.
Commentary: HOW does this source PROVE your claim? Explain the connection explicitly — never assume the reader sees it.
Second piece of evidence: a second source, or specific knowledge.
Commentary: Same — explain the WHY.
Synthesis move: How do these two pieces of evidence reinforce each other or address the same aspect of the issue from different angles?
Closing sentence: Restate the paragraph's contribution to your overall argument.
3
Body Paragraph 2 — Second Line of Reasoning
Same structure as Body 1 — different reason, different sources.
Use 1–2 additional sources (aim to use 4–5 sources total across the essay).
Commentary must explain HOW each source proves THIS specific claim — not the thesis generally.
4
Body Paragraph 3 (optional but recommended) — Concession + Refutation
Topic sentence: Acknowledge the strongest opposing argument. Use specific evidence (often a source that contradicts your position).
Refutation: Explain WHY your position still stands despite this counterargument. Does it address a different aspect? Is the counterevidence incomplete? Does it actually support a premise you agree with?
This paragraph, done well, earns the Sophistication point by demonstrating genuine complexity.
5
Conclusion (2–4 sentences)
Restate your thesis in new words (not copy-paste).
Brief statement of the larger significance: why does this issue matter beyond the sources? What are the stakes?
Optional sophistication move: Connect to a broader context, a different time period, or a related issue. This is a second opportunity for the Sophistication point.
Synthesis Citation Rules
  • Cite by label: (Source A), (Source B), etc. Or refer descriptively: 'As the infographic from the EPA shows...'
  • You can quote, paraphrase, or summarize — all count equally as source use.
  • Minimum: cite ≥ 3 sources for Row B credit. Aim for 4–5.
  • Do NOT just parade sources: 'Source A says X. Source B says Y. Source C says Z.' That's a report, not an argument.
  • Sources should support YOUR claim — you lead, they follow.
Common Synthesis Mistakes
  • Thesis that just describes the debate: 'There are many perspectives on this issue' = 0 points for thesis.
  • Source dumping: 'According to Source A, [long summary]. Source B states [long summary].' — no commentary, earns Row B at 1.
  • Commentary that summarizes instead of analyzes: 'This shows that...' vs. 'This proves that [claim] because...'
  • Forgetting to cite: Using source content without attribution. Cite even when paraphrasing.
  • Agreeing with all sources: You must take a POSITION. If you agree with all sources equally, you haven't made an argument.

FRQ 2
Rhetorical Analysis Essay — Complete Structure
~40 min · ~18%

🔍What the Rhetorical Analysis Essay Is Actually Asking

The Task
  • You are given one nonfiction passage (speech, essay, letter, editorial, etc.).
  • Your job: analyze the rhetorical choices the writer makes and explain how those choices work together to achieve the writer's purpose.
  • You are NOT analyzing what the writer says (content). You are analyzing HOW they say it (form, strategy, technique).
  • The prompt usually identifies the speaker, audience, and purpose — use that information. It's telling you what to connect your analysis to.

📐Complete Essay Structure — Rhetorical Analysis

RHETORICAL ANALYSIS ESSAY — FULL STRUCTURE
1
Introduction (4–6 sentences)
Sentence 1: Identify the speaker, the text, the context (use SPACE-CAT — especially Exigence and Context).
Sentence 2–3: Brief characterization of the speaker's rhetorical situation — what is the urgency? What does the audience believe coming in?
Sentence 4–5: THESIS — a claim about HOW specific rhetorical choices work together to achieve the writer's purpose.

Weak thesis: "The author uses many rhetorical devices to persuade the audience."
Strong thesis: "By embedding personal anecdote within a framework of historical allusion, and sustaining a tone that shifts from grief to moral resolve, [Author] compels [Audience] to move from sympathy to obligation — transforming passive readers into active agents."

Your thesis must: name 2–3 SPECIFIC choices + connect them to a SPECIFIC effect on the SPECIFIC audience.
2
Body Paragraph 1 — First Rhetorical Strategy (8–12 sentences)
Topic sentence: Name the first rhetorical choice and make a claim about its effect.
Evidence: Quote or closely paraphrase a specific passage that illustrates the choice. Keep quotes short — 1–2 lines.
Analysis Layer 1: What is the device? What does it do at the surface level?
Analysis Layer 2: WHY does this choice work on THIS audience? What does it make them feel/believe?
Analysis Layer 3: HOW does this choice serve the writer's larger purpose? Connect back to thesis.
Second example (optional): A second instance of the same strategy, or a contrasting moment that shows range.
Transition: How does this set up the next paragraph's strategy?
3
Body Paragraph 2 — Second Rhetorical Strategy
Same structure. Choose a DIFFERENT type of choice (if Para 1 was diction, consider structure or appeals here).
The best essays show how the second strategy reinforces or extends the first — they WORK TOGETHER.
Ask: "If I removed this strategy, what would be lost? What gap would appear in the persuasion?"
4
Body Paragraph 3 — Third Strategy or Synthesis Paragraph
Option A: Analyze a third rhetorical choice (tone shift, structural choice, use of kairos).
Option B (recommended for sophistication): Show how the choices from paragraphs 1 and 2 WORK TOGETHER — explain the cumulative effect on the audience. This is where Row B 4th-point logic lives. This paragraph earns the Sophistication point if done genuinely.
5
Conclusion (2–4 sentences)
Restate thesis in new language. What has your analysis revealed about this text's power?
Kairotic move (optional): Why did this text need to be written at THIS moment? What would the audience have lost without it?
Final sentence: Larger significance — what does this text's rhetoric reveal about the power of language in this context?
The 3-Layer Analysis Method
  • Every body paragraph should drill down through three layers:
  • Layer 1 — IDENTIFY: Name the device and point to the specific textual evidence. ('In the third paragraph, [author] uses anaphora...')
  • Layer 2 — EXPLAIN EFFECT: What does this choice do to the reader? What emotion, belief, or reaction does it create? ('...creating a drumbeat of urgency that forces the reader to feel each accusation as a separate moral weight...')
  • Layer 3 — CONNECT TO PURPOSE: How does this effect serve the writer's larger argument or goal? Connect back to your thesis. ('...which positions the audience not as passive observers but as morally responsible actors who share in the urgency.')
  • The test: Could your commentary apply to any essay, or only THIS essay? Generic commentary ('this makes it more persuasive') = Layer 1. Specific commentary about THIS audience, THIS moment, THIS choice = Layer 3.
Common Rhetorical Analysis Mistakes
  • Listing devices without analysis: 'The author uses alliteration, hyperbole, and rhetorical questions.' — Row B = 0.
  • Analyzing content, not craft: 'The author argues that climate change is real.' — That's summary, not analysis.
  • Thesis that names strategies but not their effect: 'The author uses ethos, pathos, and logos to persuade.' — Row A = 0 (no line of reasoning about HOW or to WHAT effect).
  • Treating all devices as equal: Some choices are structural; some are local. The strongest essays explain which choices carry the most persuasive weight and WHY.
  • Forgetting audience: The effect of a rhetorical choice is only meaningful relative to the specific audience. Always name the audience and explain why THIS choice works on THEM.

FRQ 3
Argument Essay — Complete Structure
~40 min · ~18%

💡What the Argument Essay Is Actually Asking

The Task
  • You are given a prompt that presents a debatable claim or question about a broad topic (technology, education, success, conformity, justice, etc.).
  • Your job: take a defensible position and support it using your own evidence — from history, literature, current events, science, your own experience.
  • No sources are provided. You must bring the evidence yourself.
  • The strongest Argument essays don't just agree or disagree — they make a nuanced argument that acknowledges complexity while still defending a clear position.

📐Complete Essay Structure — Argument

ARGUMENT ESSAY — FULL STRUCTURE
1
Introduction (4–6 sentences)
Sentence 1: Hook — a specific, surprising fact, historical moment, or thought experiment that creates context.
Sentence 2–3: Frame the debate — what is the tension? What do most people assume? What is the underlying question?
Sentence 4–5: THESIS — your defensible position, with a line of reasoning.

Weak thesis: "Social media has both positive and negative effects."
Strong thesis: "Although social media platforms have democratized information access, their algorithmic architecture systematically amplifies outrage and tribalism at the cost of nuanced discourse — making them a net threat to deliberative democracy."

Your thesis must: take a CLEAR POSITION + give the REASON(S) — not just name the topic.
2
Body Paragraph 1 — First Reason + Specific Evidence (8–12 sentences)
Topic sentence: First reason your position is correct — one specific, arguable claim.
Evidence 1: Introduce your first specific example. Name the event, work, person, study, law, etc.
Commentary 1: HOW does this prove your claim? What is the causal or logical link?
Evidence 2 (optional): A second example that reinforces from a different angle.
Commentary 2: Same — link explicitly to the claim.
Synthesis: What does the accumulation of this evidence prove about your argument's core claim?

Specificity test: Can your evidence be fact-checked? "Studies show" = too vague. "A 2017 American Psychological Association study linking Instagram use to adolescent depression" = specific enough.
3
Body Paragraph 2 — Second Reason + Evidence
Same structure. A different reason supporting your thesis, with different evidence.
Best essays show that the second reason is not just another example of the first — it addresses a DIFFERENT dimension of the issue.
4
Body Paragraph 3 — Concession and Refutation (REQUIRED for full score)
Concession: Acknowledge the strongest version of the counterargument. Be generous — steelman it. Name the specific objection.
Pivot: "However..." / "This objection, while valid in limited contexts..." / "Yet this analysis overlooks..."
Refutation: Explain specifically why your position still stands. Don't just dismiss — engage. Show that your argument accounts for the counterargument's best points while still being more compelling.

Why this matters: A concession-refutation paragraph demonstrates that you understand the issue's complexity, which is the path to the Sophistication point. It also builds ethos — fair acknowledgment of opposition signals intellectual confidence, not weakness.
5
Conclusion (3–5 sentences)
Restate thesis in new language — don't copy your introduction.
Broader significance: Why does this issue matter beyond the prompt? What are the stakes for society, democracy, education, human flourishing?
Optional sophistication move: Connect to a longer historical arc, an emerging trend, or a philosophical tradition that situates your argument in a larger conversation.
Final sentence: Leave the reader with something to think about — a question, an image, or a call to action (but only if it serves the argument).
Evidence Bank — What Counts as Specific Evidence
  • History: Named events with dates and specific outcomes (not 'throughout history...').
  • Literature: Named work + author + specific character/scene/quote and its relevance.
  • Science: Named study/researcher/year + specific finding.
  • Current events: Named legislation, organization, leader, or policy with specific details.
  • Personal experience: Specific enough to be credible — not 'I once felt...' but a concrete situation with specific stakes.
  • Philosophy: Named philosopher + specific concept (Rawls' veil of ignorance, Bentham's utility calculus).
  • Red flags: 'Studies show,' 'Many experts agree,' 'Throughout history,' 'In today's society,' 'As we all know' — vague = no Row B credit.
Common Argument Essay Mistakes
  • Thesis that hedges: 'Social media can be both good and bad depending on how you use it' = 0 for thesis. Take a position.
  • Vague evidence: 'The French Revolution shows that...' — which aspect? Which event? Which outcome? Name it specifically.
  • Commentary that restates instead of analyzes: 'This proves that social media is bad.' Full stop. HOW? WHY? What is the mechanism?
  • No counterargument: An essay that never acknowledges opposition earns maximum Row B = 3 and almost certainly no Row C.
  • Confusing your experience with universal truth: Personal anecdotes work as evidence, but must be generalized carefully. 'I saw this in my school' needs to connect to a broader claim.
  • Performative sophistication: Starting with 'Webster's Dictionary defines...' or ending with 'In conclusion, as I have demonstrated...' signals formulaic thinking.

1. A student's argument thesis reads: 'The standardized testing system in American education has serious flaws that need to be addressed.' Why does this fail to earn Row A?
  • A) It's too long
  • B) It makes a factual claim that nearly everyone would agree with, not a defensible position with a line of reasoning
  • C) It should mention specific tests by name
  • D) Theses should not critique institutions
B is correct. A defensible thesis must be contestable — someone must be able to reasonably disagree. 'Testing has flaws' is basically agreed upon universally; there's no argument here. A thesis with a line of reasoning would be: 'Standardized testing does not measure intelligence or future success so much as it measures access to test preparation — making it less an academic equalizer than a formalization of economic advantage.'
2. The concession-refutation paragraph in the Argument Essay primarily demonstrates:
  • A) That the student has read the sources carefully
  • B) The sophistication to acknowledge genuine complexity while maintaining a defensible position — the path to Row C
  • C) Knowledge of rhetorical devices
  • D) That the essay has sufficient length
B is correct. The concession-refutation move is the clearest path to the Sophistication point because it requires genuinely engaging with the complexity of the issue. You can't steelman the opposition and fake it — a reader can tell. Done well, it elevates the entire essay from a one-sided argument to a genuinely reasoned position.
Perfect Score Checklist
  • Row A (1 pt): defensible CLAIM with a LINE OF REASONING — not a fact, not a plan to argue
  • Row B (4 pts): specific evidence + commentary explaining HOW it proves the claim — for ALL claims
  • Row C (1 pt): sophistication throughout — nuanced argument, broader context, or vivid style
  • SPACE-CAT: Speaker, Purpose, Audience, Context, Exigence, Choices, Appeals, Tone
  • Kairos = appeal of timing — the argument is powerful specifically BECAUSE of when/where it is made
  • Four appeals: Ethos (credibility), Pathos (emotion), Logos (logic), Kairos (timing/context)
  • 3-Layer analysis: IDENTIFY device → EXPLAIN effect on audience → CONNECT to writer's purpose
  • Synthesis: cite ≥3 sources with commentary linking each to YOUR argument — not a source report
  • Rhetorical analysis thesis: names SPECIFIC choices + connects to SPECIFIC effect on SPECIFIC audience
  • Argument thesis: takes CLEAR POSITION + gives the REASON(S) — not just 'there are pros and cons'
  • Concession-refutation in Argument = strongest path to Sophistication point
  • Body paragraph 3 in Rhetorical Analysis: show how strategies work TOGETHER = Row B 4th point
