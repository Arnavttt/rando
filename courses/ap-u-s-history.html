APUSH Themes (NAT WXT GEO MIG POL WOR ARC SOC)
NAT – National Identity
How American identity has evolved across groups, regions, and time. Immigration, citizenship, assimilation, pluralism.
WXT – Work, Exchange, Technology
Labor systems, economic change, technological innovation, trade, capitalism, and industrialization.
GEO – Geography & Environment
How geography shapes migration, settlement, and development. Environmental consequences of human activity.
MIG – Migration & Settlement
Internal and external migration patterns. Push/pull factors, urbanization, frontier expansion.
POL – Politics & Power
Government structure, political parties, reform movements, civil rights, constitutional development.
WOR – America in the World
Diplomatic relations, foreign policy, wars, colonialism, imperialism, role in global affairs.
ARC – American & Regional Culture
Cultural movements, religion, arts, literature, ideologies, regional identities.
SOC – Social Structures
Class, race, gender, family, and social mobility. How social hierarchies are created and challenged.

Historical Thinking Skills & Reasoning Processes
4 Historical Reasoning Processes (Tested on Every Essay)
Causation:
Explain causes and effects of historical events. Must distinguish between immediate causes and longer-term causes.
Comparison:
Identify similarities and differences between historical developments, periods, or regions.
Continuity and Change Over Time (CCOT):
Explain what changed and what stayed the same across a time period.
Contextualization:
Connect a specific event/development to broader historical patterns occurring before, during, or after.

Current DBQ Rubric — 7 Points Total
DBQ — Document-Based Question
7 points
1 pt
Thesis/Claim:
Responds to the prompt with a historically defensible thesis that establishes a line of reasoning. Must be more than a restatement of the prompt. Can appear in intro OR conclusion.
1 pt
Contextualization:
Describes a broader historical context accurately and connects it to the argument. Must be more than a phrase — needs to be a developed description of events before, during, or after the time frame.
1 pt
Evidence — Documents:
Accurately uses content from at least 3 documents to address the topic of the prompt.
1 pt
Evidence — Documents (higher):
Uses content from at least 4 documents AND supports an argument in response to the prompt.
1 pt
Evidence — Beyond Docs:
Uses at least one additional piece of specific historical evidence (not in documents) relevant to the argument.
1 pt
Analysis — Sourcing (HIPP):
For at least 2 documents, explains how/why the document's
Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, or Point of view
is relevant to the argument.
1 pt
Analysis — Complexity:
Demonstrates complex understanding through corroboration, qualification, or modification of the argument. Using ALL 7 docs counts. Connecting to a different period, geographic area, or theme counts.
The Heimler DBQ Thesis Formula
1
Read the prompt.
Identify the time period, the exact question asked, and the reasoning process required (causation? comparison? CCOT?)
2
Scan all 7 documents
in 10–12 minutes. Annotate each with HIPP: Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, Point of view.
3
Group documents
into 2–3 categories that will form your body paragraph arguments. Your groups should support a unified thesis.
4
Write your thesis
using the Heimler formula:
"Although [counterpoint/nuance], [your line of reasoning], because [evidence group 1], [evidence group 2], and [evidence group 3]."
5
Write contextualization
in a separate paragraph BEFORE your thesis: describe 2–3 sentences about the broader historical backdrop that existed BEFORE the prompt's time frame.
6
Write body paragraphs:
Each uses docs + outside evidence + at least one HIPP sourcing analysis.
7
Complexity:
In your conclusion or throughout, connect your argument to another time period, region, or use all 7 docs.

LEQ — Long Essay Question
6 points
1 pt
Thesis/Claim:
Historically defensible thesis with a line of reasoning. Cannot simply restate or rephrase the prompt.
1 pt
Contextualization:
Describes broader historical context and connects it to the argument. Developed description — not just a phrase.
1 pt

Provides at least two specific examples of evidence relevant to the topic.
1 pt
Evidence — Supports Argument:
Uses specific evidence to support an argument in response to the prompt.

1 pt

Demonstrates complex understanding by corroborating, qualifying, or modifying the argument using diverse evidence or perspectives.

SAQ Rubric — 3 Points Each
SAQ Structure (A, B, C = 1 point each)
Each SAQ part is worth exactly 1 point. There is no thesis required.
Identify/Describe tasks:
One sentence is usually sufficient — name the thing and say something accurate about it.
Explain tasks:
Requires a sentence PLUS reasoning that connects evidence to a historical claim. Ask yourself: 'So what? Why did this matter?'
Write in complete sentences. Bullet points are acceptable but complete sentences are safer.
Be
specific
— vague answers like 'it caused problems' earn nothing. Name the people, events, and dates.

Period 1 (1491–1607)
Pre-Columbian through Early Contact
4–6% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Native American Diversity
Hundreds of distinct nations — Aztec/Inca (complex empires), Eastern Woodlands (agriculture + hunting), Plains (nomadic buffalo hunting), Pacific Northwest (fishing). Environment shaped political and social structures.
European Motivations (3 G's)
God (religious conversion), Gold (wealth/trade routes), Glory (national prestige). Spain, Portugal, France, England competed. Columbus 1492 — accident seeking Asia.
Columbian Exchange
EUROPE→AMERICAS: horses, cattle, wheat, sugar, smallpox (devastating), measles, influenza. AMERICAS→EUROPE: corn, potatoes, tomatoes, tobacco, cacao. Demographic catastrophe for indigenous peoples — up to 90% population loss in some regions.
Spanish Encomienda System
Labor system: Spanish colonizers given authority over indigenous people in exchange for military service. Led to enslavement, forced labor, population collapse. Las Casas documented abuses — early critique of colonialism.

1. The Columbian Exchange most significantly impacted the Americas through:
A) Introduction of democratic government
B) Spread of European diseases that devastated indigenous populations
C) Arrival of European manufactured goods
D) Introduction of new agricultural techniques
B is correct.
European diseases — especially smallpox — killed an estimated 50–90% of indigenous populations in many regions. This was the single most significant consequence of first contact, enabling later conquest and colonization.
2. The encomienda system established by Spain in the Americas was primarily:
A) A system of free trade between Spain and indigenous nations
B) A labor system granting colonizers authority over indigenous people in exchange for military service
C) A religious mission system for converting Native Americans
D) A land grant system for Spanish soldiers
B is correct.
The encomienda gave Spanish colonizers (encomenderos) authority over a number of indigenous people who owed labor or tribute. It effectively created a system of forced labor and is closely associated with the demographic collapse of indigenous populations.

British Colonies: Regional Differences
New England: Puritan theocracy, small farms, fishing/trade, town meetings. Middle: diverse religions/ethnicities, wheat farming, artisan economy. Southern: tobacco/rice/indigo plantations, headright system, indentured servants → enslaved Africans.
Chesapeake vs. New England
Chesapeake (Virginia, Maryland): high death rate, male-dominated, tobacco economy, indentured servants. New England: religious community, family migration, longer lives, diversified economy.
Bacon's Rebellion (1676)
Nathaniel Bacon led frontier farmers against Governor Berkeley's Native American policy. Revealed tension between wealthy planters and poor whites → Virginia planters shifted from indentured servants to enslaved Africans.
Salutary Neglect
British policy of not strictly enforcing colonial trade laws (Navigation Acts). Created de facto colonial self-governance. When Britain reversed course (1763), colonists resisted — setting stage for Revolution.
Great Awakening (1730s–1740s)
Religious revival — Whitefield, Edwards. Challenged established churches, emphasized individual conversion. Democratized religion, fostered spirit of questioning authority → contributed to Revolutionary ideology.

A) The establishment of representative government

B is correct.

Period 3 (1754–1800)
Revolution & Republic
10–17% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Road to Revolution
French & Indian War (1754–63) → Britain needs revenue → Stamp Act (1765), Townshend Acts (1767), Tea Act (1773). Colonists: 'No taxation without representation.' Boston Massacre (1770), Tea Party (1773), Intolerable Acts (1774).
Declaration of Independence (1776)
Locke's natural rights theory: life, liberty, property. 'All men are created equal' — revolutionary claim, though limited in practice. Listed grievances against King George III. Legitimized Revolution with Enlightenment philosophy.
Articles of Confederation (1781–1789)
First government: weak central authority, no power to tax or regulate commerce, no executive or judicial branch. Failed to handle Shays' Rebellion (1786). Led to Constitutional Convention (1787).
Constitution & Bill of Rights
Federalism: divided sovereignty between national/state. Separation of powers. Great Compromise: bicameral legislature. 3/5 Compromise. Ratification debate: Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay — Federalist Papers) vs. Anti-Federalists (feared tyranny). Bill of Rights (1791) addressed Anti-Federalist concerns.
Hamilton vs. Jefferson
Hamilton: strong federal government, national bank, manufacturing economy, loose construction. Jefferson: states' rights, agrarian republic, strict construction. First American political party system.

Hamilton's Report on Manufactures, national bank, assumption of state debts, and protective tariffs all aimed to build American industrial and financial power. Jefferson opposed this as threatening republican virtue and state sovereignty.
Period 4 (1800–1848)
Democracy & Expansion
10–17% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Jeffersonian Democracy
'Revolution of 1800' — peaceful transfer of power. Jefferson reduced federal power, cut military, but expanded federal authority with Louisiana Purchase (1803). Lewis & Clark. Marbury v. Madison (1803) established judicial review.
Market Revolution
Early 1800s–1840s: Interchangeable parts, textile mills (Lowell System), Erie Canal (1825), railroads. Created national market economy. Transformed labor: factory work, 'Cult of Domesticity,' free Black workers in North.
Jacksonian Democracy
Andrew Jackson (1829–1837): expanded voting rights for white men, spoils system, Indian Removal Act (1830) → Trail of Tears. Destroyed Second Bank of US. Nullification Crisis with South Carolina (1832).
Antebellum Reform Movements
Second Great Awakening fueled reform. Temperance (WCTU), prison reform, public education (Horace Mann), women's rights (Seneca Falls 1848 — Declaration of Sentiments), abolitionism (Garrison — radical; Douglass — lived experience; ACS — colonization).
Manifest Destiny & Expansion
John O'Sullivan coined term 1845. Texas Annexation (1845), Oregon Territory (1846), Mexican-American War (1846–48) → Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo. Wilmot Proviso — should slavery expand? Intensified sectional crisis.

The Market Revolution — driven by canals, railroads, and industrial technology — created a national economy. Northern factory workers became wage laborers. Southern cotton fed Northern mills. This interdependence (with underlying tensions) was a major feature of antebellum America.

Period 5 (1844–1877)
Civil War & Reconstruction
10–17% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Causes of Civil War (SCALED)
Slavery (core issue), Compromise failures (Missouri Compromise struck down), Abolitionism intensifying, Lincoln's election (1860), Economic differences (industrial North vs. agrarian slave South), Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857) — enslaved people have no citizenship rights.
Civil War Military & Political
Union advantages: industrial capacity, railroads, navy, population. Emancipation Proclamation (Jan 1, 1863) — freed enslaved people in rebel states; changed war's meaning; prevented European recognition of Confederacy. 54th Massachusetts Infantry. 13th Amendment (1865).
Reconstruction (1865–1877)
13th (abolition), 14th (citizenship, equal protection), 15th (Black male voting) Amendments. Freedmen's Bureau. Black Codes. Sharecropping replaced slavery economically. Radical Republicans vs. Johnson. Compromise of 1877 ended Reconstruction.
Reconstruction's End
Ku Klux Klan terrorized Black voters. Supreme Court weakened 14th Amendment (Slaughterhouse Cases, Civil Rights Cases). Compromise of 1877: Hayes wins presidency, federal troops withdrawn from South. 'Redeemer' Democrats restore white supremacy. Sharecropping, convict leasing, Jim Crow began.

0/0 correct
Reset
1. The Emancipation Proclamation (1863) was significant primarily because:
A) It immediately freed all enslaved people in the United States
B) It transformed the Civil War into a war against slavery, preventing European recognition of the Confederacy and allowing Black men to enlist
C) It gave formerly enslaved people full citizenship and voting rights

B is correct.
The Proclamation only freed enslaved people in rebel states (not border states), and was unenforceable in Confederate territory. Its true significance: it changed the war's ideological stakes to include freedom, made European support for the Confederacy politically impossible, and opened the door for 180,000+ Black soldiers to fight for the Union.
2. Reconstruction ended primarily because:
A) All formerly enslaved people had achieved economic and political equality
B) The Compromise of 1877 withdrew federal troops, allowing Southern 'Redeemer' governments to dismantle Black political gains through terror and law
C) The Supreme Court ruled Reconstruction unconstitutional
D) President Grant ended federal involvement to focus on western expansion
B is correct.
The Compromise of 1877 resolved the disputed Hayes-Tilden election by giving Hayes the presidency in exchange for withdrawing federal troops from the South. Without federal enforcement, Ku Klux Klan violence, poll taxes, grandfather clauses, and literacy tests effectively ended Black political participation for decades.
Period 6 (1865–1898)
Gilded Age & Industrialization
10–17% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Industrial Revolution (2nd)
Steel (Bessemer process), railroads, oil (Rockefeller/Standard Oil), electricity (Edison), telephone (Bell). Robber Barons vs. Captains of Industry debate. Trusts and monopolies. Social Darwinism justified inequality.
Labor Movement
Knights of Labor (all workers), AFL (Gompers — skilled workers, bread-and-butter unionism). Homestead Strike (1892), Pullman Strike (1894 — Debs, federal injunction). Child labor, 12-hour days, unsafe conditions.
Gilded Age Politics
Political machines (Tammany Hall — Tweed). Pendleton Civil Service Act (1883) — merit system. Farmers: Grange Movement → Populist Party (People's Party) 1892 — William Jennings Bryan, free silver, graduated income tax, direct election of senators.
New South & Jim Crow
Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) — 'separate but equal.' Sharecropping, convict leasing. Ida B. Wells (anti-lynching). Booker T. Washington (accommodation, Atlanta Compromise) vs. W.E.B. Du Bois (civil rights NOW, Talented Tenth).
Western Expansion
Homestead Act (1862). Transcontinental Railroad (1869). Destruction of buffalo. Dawes Act (1887) — assimilation, allotment of reservation lands, destroyed tribal sovereignty. Ghost Dance. Wounded Knee (1890). Frederick Jackson Turner's Frontier Thesis (1893).

1. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) is most significant because:
A) It ended racial segregation in public schools
B) It established the 'separate but equal' doctrine, providing constitutional justification for Jim Crow segregation for 58 years
C) It gave formerly enslaved people full voting rights

Board of Education (1954). Justice Harlan's dissent — calling the Constitution 'color-blind' — became the basis for the later civil rights argument.
2. The Populist (People's) Party of the 1890s primarily represented:
A) Urban factory workers seeking higher wages
B) Farmers and debtors seeking to address economic inequality through government intervention — free silver, graduated income tax, railroad regulation
C) Industrialists seeking protective tariffs
D) Progressive middle-class reformers
B is correct.
Populists were mostly Southern and Western farmers crushed by falling crop prices, high railroad rates, and tight credit. Their platform — free coinage of silver (inflation to help debtors), direct election of senators, graduated income tax — challenged Gilded Age laissez-faire orthodoxy. Many Populist ideas were later adopted by Progressives.
Period 7 (1890–1945)
Progressivism through WWII
10–17% of exam
🏛️
Key Concepts
Progressive Era (1900–1920)
Muckrakers: Upton Sinclair (The Jungle), Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), Jacob Riis (How the Other Half Lives). Reforms: Pure Food & Drug Act, Sherman Antitrust Act enforcement (TR), 16th (income tax), 17th (direct Senate election), 18th (Prohibition), 19th (women's suffrage) Amendments.
WWI & its Aftermath
US neutral 1914–17. Zimmermann Telegram + unrestricted submarine warfare → US enters 1917. Wilson's 14 Points, League of Nations. Senate rejected League (isolationism). Red Scare, Palmer Raids. Nativist backlash: Emergency Quota Act (1921), National Origins Act (1924).
1920s
Harlem Renaissance (Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston). Great Migration of Black Americans North. Jazz Age. Consumerism (installment buying, automobiles). Fundamentalism vs. Modernism (Scopes Trial 1925). KKK revival. Women's changed roles.
Great Depression & New Deal
Stock market crash 1929. Hoover's 'voluntarism' failed. FDR's New Deal: relief, recovery, reform. CCC, TVA, FDIC, Social Security Act (1935), Wagner Act (NLRA). Conservative opposition: 'court-packing' plan failed. New Deal coalition: labor unions, urban immigrants, Black Americans, Solid South.
WWII
US 'Arsenal of Democracy' — Lend-Lease. Pearl Harbor (Dec 7, 1941) → full entry. Japanese American internment (Korematsu v. US 1944). Double V Campaign. Atomic bombs: Hiroshima/Nagasaki (Aug 1945). Holocaust. UN founded. Beginning of Cold War.

Cold War & Civil Rights

Key Concepts
Cold War Origins
USSR vs. USA: ideological conflict (communism vs. capitalism/democracy). Truman Doctrine (1947): contain communism. Marshall Plan (1948): rebuild Europe. NATO (1949). Korean War (1950–53): limited war, 38th parallel.
Civil Rights Movement
Brown v. Board (1954) — 'separate but equal' unconstitutional. Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56) — King, nonviolent direct action. Great Society & Liberal Consensus
LBJ: Medicare/Medicaid, Voting Rights Act, Immigration Act (1965), War on Poverty. Warren Court: Gideon, Miranda, Roe v. Wade (1973). New Left: SDS, antiwar movement. Second-wave feminism: NOW, ERA, Roe v. Wade.
Vietnam & Counterculture
Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964) — escalation. Tet Offensive (1968) — turned public opinion. Pentagon Papers. Kent State (1970). Nixon's 'Vietnamization.' Fall of Saigon (1975). Counterculture: hippies, anti-war protests, sexual revolution, environmental movement (Earth Day 1970, EPA created).
Nixon & Détente
Nixon: opened China (1972), SALT I (détente with USSR), EPA, wage/price controls. Watergate (1972–74) — cover-up, Saturday Night Massacre, resignation. Ford pardons Nixon. Carter: Camp David Accords, Iran hostage crisis, energy crisis → Reagan Revolution.

Conservative Revolution & 21st Century

Reagan Revolution

<h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">📋</span>Current DBQ Rubric — 7 Points Total</h3><div class="rubric-box">
<div class="rb-header"><span class="rb-name">DBQ — Document-Based Question</span><span class="rb-pts">7 points</span></div>
<div class="rb-body"><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Thesis/Claim:</strong> Responds to the prompt with a historically defensible thesis that establishes a line of reasoning. Must be more than a restatement of the prompt. Can appear in intro OR conclusion.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Contextualization:</strong> Describes a broader historical context accurately and connects it to the argument. Must be more than a phrase — needs to be a developed description of events before, during, or after the time frame.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Evidence — Documents:</strong> Accurately uses content from at least 3 documents to address the topic of the prompt.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Evidence — Documents (higher):</strong> Uses content from at least 4 documents AND supports an argument in response to the prompt.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Evidence — Beyond Docs:</strong> Uses at least one additional piece of specific historical evidence (not in documents) relevant to the argument.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Analysis — Sourcing (HIPP):</strong> For at least 2 documents, explains how/why the document's <strong>Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, or Point of view</strong> is relevant to the argument.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Analysis — Complexity:</strong> Demonstrates complex understanding through corroboration, qualification, or modification of the argument. Using ALL 7 docs counts. Connecting to a different period, geographic area, or theme counts.</div></div></div>
</div><div class="step-box"><div class="step-title">The Heimler DBQ Thesis Formula</div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">1</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Read the prompt.</strong> Identify the time period, the exact question asked, and the reasoning process required (causation? comparison? CCOT?)</div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">2</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Scan all 7 documents</strong> in 10–12 minutes. Annotate each with HIPP: Historical situation, Intended audience, Purpose, Point of view.</div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">3</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Group documents</strong> into 2–3 categories that will form your body paragraph arguments. Your groups should support a unified thesis.</div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">4</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Write your thesis</strong> using the Heimler formula: <em>"Although [counterpoint/nuance], [your line of reasoning], because [evidence group 1], [evidence group 2], and [evidence group 3]."</em></div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">5</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Write contextualization</strong> in a separate paragraph BEFORE your thesis: describe 2–3 sentences about the broader historical backdrop that existed BEFORE the prompt's time frame.</div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">6</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Write body paragraphs:</strong> Each uses docs + outside evidence + at least one HIPP sourcing analysis.</div></div><div class="step"><div class="step-num">7</div><div class="step-body"><strong>Complexity:</strong> In your conclusion or throughout, connect your argument to another time period, region, or use all 7 docs.</div></div></div><h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">📋</span>Current LEQ Rubric — 6 Points</h3><div class="rubric-box">
<div class="rb-header"><span class="rb-name">LEQ — Long Essay Question</span><span class="rb-pts">6 points</span></div>
<div class="rb-body"><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Thesis/Claim:</strong> Historically defensible thesis with a line of reasoning. Cannot simply restate or rephrase the prompt.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Contextualization:</strong> Describes broader historical context and connects it to the argument. Developed description — not just a phrase.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Evidence — Specific Examples:</strong> Provides at least two specific examples of evidence relevant to the topic.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Evidence — Supports Argument:</strong> Uses specific evidence to support an argument in response to the prompt.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Analysis — Historical Reasoning:</strong> Uses comparison, causation, or CCOT to frame or structure the argument. Must be consistent throughout, not just mentioned.</div></div><div class="rb-row"><div class="rb-pts-col">1 pt</div><div class="rb-desc"><strong>Analysis — Complexity:</strong> Demonstrates complex understanding by corroborating, qualifying, or modifying the argument using diverse evidence or perspectives.</div></div></div>
</div><h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">📋</span>SAQ Rubric — 3 Points Each</h3><div class="callout"><div class="callout-lbl">SAQ Structure (A, B, C = 1 point each)</div><ul><li>Each SAQ part is worth exactly 1 point. There is no thesis required.</li><li><strong>Identify/Describe tasks:</strong> One sentence is usually sufficient — name the thing and say something accurate about it.</li><li><strong>Explain tasks:</strong> Requires a sentence PLUS reasoning that connects evidence to a historical claim. Ask yourself: 'So what? Why did this matter?'</li><li>Write in complete sentences. Bullet points are acceptable but complete sentences are safer.</li><li>Be <em>specific</em> — vague answers like 'it caused problems' earn nothing. Name the people, events, and dates.</li></ul></div><div class="quiz-section">
