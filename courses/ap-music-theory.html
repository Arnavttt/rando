Time Signatures
Top number = beats per measure. Bottom = which note gets one beat.
4/4 (common time):
4 quarter-note beats per measure.
3/4 (waltz):
3 quarter-note beats. Strong beat on beat 1.

2 groups of 3 eighth notes. Felt in 2.

Simple (2/4, 3/4, 4/4) — beat divides into 2. Compound (6/8, 9/8) — beat divides into 3.

The Major Scale & Key Signatures
Major Scale Formula: W-W-H-W-W-W-H
W = whole step (2 semitones). H = half step (1 semitone).
C major: C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C (no sharps or flats — the reference scale).
Circle of Fifths:
Moving clockwise adds one sharp. Moving counter-clockwise adds one flat.
Sharp keys: G(1#), D(2#), A(3#), E(4#), B(5#), F#(6#), C#(7#)
Flat keys: F(1♭), B♭(2♭), E♭(3♭), A♭(4♭), D♭(5♭), G♭(6♭), C♭(7♭)

1. The interval from D up to A is a:
A) Perfect 4th
B) Major 6th
C) Perfect 5th
D) Minor 6th
C: Perfect 5th.
Count the letter names inclusively: D(1)-E(2)-F(3)-G(4)-A(5) = 5th. Then check quality: D to A = 7 semitones = perfect 5th. Perfect intervals (unisons, 4ths, 5ths, octaves) come from the major scale built on the lower note.
2. In C major, the chord built on scale degree 7 is:
A) C major
B) G dominant 7th
C) B diminished triad
D) A minor
C: B diminished.
Scale degree 7 in C major = B. Build a triad: B-D-F. B→D = minor 3rd; D→F = minor 3rd. Two stacked minor thirds = diminished triad (vii°). The diminished 5th (tritone) gives it strong tension → resolves up to I.

The Absolute Voice Leading Rules — Must Not Break
Voice Leading Rules
No parallel perfect 5ths:
Two voices moving in the same direction and maintaining a P5 between them. This is the most common error.
No parallel perfect octaves:
Same principle — two voices moving in parallel octaves.
No voice crossing:
Alto should never go above soprano; tenor should never go above alto.
Resolve the leading tone (7̂):
The 7th scale degree MUST resolve up a half step to the tonic. Never double the leading tone.
Resolve the chordal 7th DOWN by step:
In a V7, the 7th of the chord (4th scale degree) must resolve DOWN by step to the 3rd scale degree (iii or I⁶).
No augmented melodic intervals:
No voice should leap an augmented 2nd, augmented 4th, etc.
Avoid large leaps:
Prefer stepwise motion. Leaps of a 6th or 7th are awkward. After a large leap, move by step in the opposite direction.

Expected I, got vi. Surprising but not unresolved — creates continuation.

1. Parallel perfect fifths are prohibited in SATB voice leading because:
A) They sound dissonant
B) They cause two independent voices to lose independence — merging into one sonority instead of two distinct lines
C) They violate church rules from the Middle Ages only
D) They are too hard to sing
B is correct.
The prohibition on parallel 5ths is about voice independence — two voices moving in parallel perfect consonances stop functioning as two lines and merge. 18th-century common practice harmony requires four clearly independent voices, each with its own melodic logic.
2. A V7 chord resolves to I. The chordal 7th (4̂) must move:
A) Up a half step to 5̂
B) Down a step to 3̂
C) Stay as a common tone
D) Jump down a 4th
B: Down a step to 3̂.
The chordal 7th creates a dissonance (tritone with the 3rd of the V chord) that demands resolution. The 7th resolves down by step — from 4̂ to 3̂. Simultaneously, the leading tone (7̂) resolves UP to 1̂. These opposing resolutions drive the V7→I progression.

Rondo

Main theme (A) alternates with episodes. Often last movement of concertos.
Sonata Form
Exposition–Development–Recapitulation
Most important Classical form. Exposition: 2 themes, different keys. Development: fragmented, modulating. Recap: both themes in tonic.

1. Sonata form's 'development' section is characterized by:
A) Presentation of two contrasting themes in stable keys
B) Return of both themes in the tonic key
C) Fragmentation and modulation of themes through multiple keys — harmonic instability
D) A new, contrasting melody
C is correct.
The development section takes thematic material from the exposition and develops it: fragments, sequences, inverts, and passes it through various keys. It creates maximum harmonic tension before the recapitulation's return to stability in the tonic.
2. A deceptive cadence resolves V to:
A) I
B) IV
C) vii°
D) vi
D: vi.
The deceptive cadence substitutes vi (tonic minor parallel) for the expected I. The bass moves to vi, but the soprano may still resolve normally. It creates a sense of continuation rather than full closure — composers use it to extend phrases.
Perfect Score Checklist
Major scale formula: W-W-H-W-W-W-H
Circle of fifths: clockwise adds sharps. Counter-clockwise adds flats.
Sharp key order: F C G D A E B (Fat Cats Go Down Alleys Eating Bugs)
Flat key order: B E A D G C F (Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles's Father)
Diatonic triads in major: I ii iii IV V vi vii° (M m m M M m dim)
SATB absolute rules: no parallel 5ths/8ths, no voice crossing, resolve leading tone UP
Chordal 7th (V7 → I): 4̂ resolves DOWN to 3̂. Leading tone 7̂ resolves UP to 1̂.
PAC = V→I root position, soprano on 1̂. HC = ends on V.
Deceptive cadence: V→vi (expected I, got vi)
Sonata form: Exposition (2 themes) → Development (fragmented/modulating) → Recapitulation (both themes, tonic)

<h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">🎵</span>The 4-Voice Texture: SATB</h3><div class="callout"><div class="callout-lbl">Voice Ranges</div><ul><li><strong>Soprano (S):</strong> C4–G5 (middle C to high G)</li><li><strong>Alto (A):</strong> G3–C5</li><li><strong>Tenor (T):</strong> C3–G4</li><li><strong>Bass (B):</strong> E2–C4</li><li>Voices should generally stay in range. Crossing voices (alto above soprano) is a <strong>voice-crossing error</strong>.</li></ul></div><h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">🎵</span>The Absolute Voice Leading Rules — Must Not Break</h3><div class="warn"><div class="warn-lbl">Voice Leading Rules</div><ul><li><strong>No parallel perfect 5ths:</strong> Two voices moving in the same direction and maintaining a P5 between them. This is the most common error.</li><li><strong>No parallel perfect octaves:</strong> Same principle — two voices moving in parallel octaves.</li><li><strong>No voice crossing:</strong> Alto should never go above soprano; tenor should never go above alto.</li><li><strong>Resolve the leading tone (7̂):</strong> The 7th scale degree MUST resolve up a half step to the tonic. Never double the leading tone.</li><li><strong>Resolve the chordal 7th DOWN by step:</strong> In a V7, the 7th of the chord (4th scale degree) must resolve DOWN by step to the 3rd scale degree (iii or I⁶).</li><li><strong>No augmented melodic intervals:</strong> No voice should leap an augmented 2nd, augmented 4th, etc.</li><li><strong>Avoid large leaps:</strong> Prefer stepwise motion. Leaps of a 6th or 7th are awkward. After a large leap, move by step in the opposite direction.</li></ul></div><h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">🎵</span>Cadence Types</h3><table class="ctable"><tr><th>Cadence</th><th>Chords</th><th>Feel/Function</th></tr><tr><td>Perfect Authentic Cadence (PAC)</td><td>V (or V7) → I, both in root position, soprano on tonic</td><td>Strongest ending — full stop. Required for period phrase structure.</td></tr><tr><td>Imperfect Authentic Cadence (IAC)</td><td>V → I, but inverted or soprano not on tonic</td><td>Cadence, but weaker — less final.</td></tr><tr><td>Half Cadence (HC)</td><td>Any chord → V</td><td>Ending on dominant — feels like a question, pause in middle.</td></tr><tr><td>Plagal Cadence</td><td>IV → I</td><td>'Amen' cadence. Soft, devotional. Often after a PAC.</td></tr><tr><td>Deceptive Cadence</td><td>V → vi</td><td>Expected I, got vi. <div class="q-text">2. A V7 chord resolves to I. The chordal 7th (4̂) must move:</div>
<ul class="q-choices"><li class="q-choice" data-correct="false" onclick="pick(this,false,'mu_v_q1')">A) Up a half step to 5̂</li>
<li class="q-choice" data-correct="true" onclick="pick(this,true,'mu_v_q1')">B) Down a step to 3̂</li>
<li class="q-choice" data-correct="false" onclick="pick(this,false,'mu_v_q1')">C) Stay as a common tone</li>
<li class="q-choice" data-correct="false" onclick="pick(this,false,'mu_v_q1')">D) Jump down a 4th</li>

<div class="q-explain show" id="exp-mu_v_q1" style="display:none"><strong>B: Down a step to 3̂.</strong> The chordal 7th creates a dissonance (tritone with the 3rd of the V chord) that demands resolution. function resetQuiz(btn) {
  const quiz = btn.closest('.quiz-section');
  quiz.querySelectorAll('.q-item').forEach(q => {
    q.classList.remove('answered');

  });
