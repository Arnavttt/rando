AP Environmental Science

This path treats APES as systems science. Each lesson asks: what is the system, what are the inputs/outputs, what human action changes it, what data reveals the change, and what solution has tradeoffs?

Prerequisites

  • Basic biology/ecology vocabulary
  • Percent/rate/unit calculations
  • Graph and map interpretation

Course skills

  • Explain environmental concepts, cycles, systems, and human impacts.
  • Analyze graphs, maps, models, food webs, energy diagrams, and data tables.
  • Calculate rates, percentages, population growth, energy efficiency, LD50, dose, and pollutant quantities.
  • Design investigations and evaluate environmental solutions.
  • Use evidence to compare costs, benefits, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences.

Units 1–2
Ecosystems & Biogeochemical Cycles
6–8% of exam
🌿
Energy Flow & Food Webs
Concept
Details
10% Rule
Only ~10% of energy transfers between trophic levels. 90% lost as heat, movement, waste.
Trophic Levels
Producers → Primary consumers → Secondary → Tertiary. Each level 10× less energy.
Gross vs. Net Productivity
GPP = total photosynthesis. NPP = GPP minus plant respiration. NPP available to consumers.
Keystone Species
Disproportionate ecosystem impact relative to biomass. Sea otters: eat urchins → protect kelp forests.
🌿
Biogeochemical Cycles
Carbon Cycle
Carbon in atmosphere (CO₂) → photosynthesis → organic matter → respiration/decomposition → back to atmosphere. FAST cycle: days–years. SLOW cycle: fossil fuel formation (millions of years). Combustion of fossil fuels = excess CO₂ → greenhouse effect.
Nitrogen Cycle
N₂ (unusable) → nitrogen fixation (bacteria, lightning) → NH₃ → nitrification → NO₃⁻ (usable) → plant uptake → denitrification → N₂. Human disruption: synthetic fertilizers (Haber-Bosch), runoff → eutrophication.
Phosphorus Cycle
No atmospheric component. Weathering releases phosphate from rock → soil/water → plants/animals → decomposition. Runoff from fertilizers = eutrophication. Mined as fertilizer — non-renewable on human timescale.
Water Cycle
Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration, groundwater. Deforestation reduces transpiration and increases surface runoff.

1. A lake receives excess phosphorus and nitrogen from agricultural runoff. The MOST LIKELY consequence is:
A) Increased fish populations
B) Eutrophication — algae blooms deplete oxygen → hypoxic dead zones
C) Higher water clarity
D) Decreased plant growth
B: Eutrophication.
Excess nutrients → algae bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them → bacteria use all O₂ → fish die (hypoxic/anoxic zone). The Gulf of Mexico dead zone is driven by Mississippi River agricultural runoff.
2. The Haber-Bosch process most directly affects which biogeochemical cycle?
A) Carbon cycle
B) Phosphorus cycle
C) Nitrogen cycle
D) Water cycle
C: Nitrogen cycle.
Haber-Bosch industrially fixes atmospheric N₂ into NH₃ (ammonia) for synthetic fertilizers. This has dramatically increased food production but also caused massive disruption of the nitrogen cycle through runoff, eutrophication, and N₂O (nitrous oxide) — a potent greenhouse gas.

Population Ecology — Key Equations
Rule of 70 (doubling time):
Doubling time (years) = 70 / annual growth rate (%)
Example: 2% growth rate → 70/2 = 35 years to double
Population growth rate:
% growth rate = [(birth rate − death rate) / 1000] × 100
(or add immigration / subtract emigration for total change)
Total Fertility Rate (TFR):
TFR < 2.1 → population eventually DECREASES (below replacement)
TFR = 2.1 → stable population
TFR > 2.1 → population GROWS

1. A country has a birth rate of 35/1000 and a death rate of 10/1000. Its annual growth rate is:
A) 3.5%
B) 2.5%
C) 10%
D) 0.025%
B: 2.5%.
(Birth rate − Death rate)/1000 × 100 = (35−10)/1000 × 100 = 25/1000 × 100 = 2.5%. Doubling time = 70/2.5 = 28 years.
2. A country in Stage 2 of the DTM is characterized by:
A) Low birth and death rates
B) High birth rates and rapidly falling death rates → rapid population growth
C) Below-replacement birth rates
D) Both high birth and death rates
B is correct.
Stage 2 begins when death rates decline (due to improved sanitation, medicine, food supply) while birth rates remain high — creating rapid natural population increase.

Biodiversity Threats — HIPPCO
HIPPCO — Major Causes of Biodiversity Loss (in order of impact)
H
— Habitat destruction (by far the #1 cause — deforestation, wetland draining, urban sprawl)
I
— Invasive species (displace natives; no natural predators; e.g., zebra mussels, kudzu, brown tree snake)
P
— Population growth (driving all other pressures through resource demand)
P
— Pollution (water, air, soil; includes light and noise pollution; endocrine disruptors)
C
— Climate change (shifting ranges; phenology mismatches; coral bleaching; sea level rise)
O
— Overexploitation (overfishing, poaching, bushmeat hunting)
🌿
Pollution — Key Distinctions
Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants
Primary: emitted directly (CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates from combustion). Secondary: formed from reactions in atmosphere (ozone O₃, NO₂, photochemical smog, acid rain H₂SO₄).
Climate Change vs. Ozone Depletion
Different problems, different causes!
Climate: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, water vapor trap heat (greenhouse effect). Ozone: CFCs destroy stratospheric O₃ → UV exposure. Fix: Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFCs. Climate: Paris Agreement (2015) — nonbinding emissions targets.
Point vs. Nonpoint Pollution
Point: identifiable single source (factory pipe, sewage outfall — regulated under Clean Water Act). Nonpoint: diffuse sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater) — much harder to regulate.
Eutrophication
Excess N or P → algae bloom → decomposers consume O₂ → hypoxic zone. Prevented by: buffer strips, wetlands, reduced fertilizer use, tertiary wastewater treatment.

1. The Montreal Protocol (1987) addressed which environmental problem?
A) Acid rain
B) Climate change
C) Stratospheric ozone depletion by CFCs
D) Eutrophication
C: Ozone depletion.
CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) catalytically destroy stratospheric ozone (O₃). The Montreal Protocol banned CFCs — it is considered the most successful international environmental treaty. The ozone layer is slowly recovering.
Do NOT confuse with the Paris Agreement
(2015, climate change).
2. A farmer applies excess nitrogen fertilizer. Rank the most likely sequence of events:
A) Runoff → eutrophication → algae bloom → hypoxia → fish kills
B) Runoff → fish kills → algae bloom → ozone depletion
C) Algae bloom → acid rain → hypoxia
D) Volatilization → greenhouse effect → drought
A is correct.
Classic eutrophication sequence: excess nutrients → algae bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them → bacteria consume dissolved O₂ → hypoxic conditions → fish and other aquatic life die. The Gulf Dead Zone is the signature APES example.

Climate Science Fundamentals
Greenhouse Effect & Climate Change
Greenhouse gases:
CO₂ (fossil fuels, deforestation), CH₄ (livestock, rice, landfills, natural gas), N₂O (fertilizers, combustion), H₂O, CFCs.
Albedo feedback:
Melting ice → less reflective surface → more heat absorbed → more warming.
Ocean acidification:
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → lower ocean pH → dissolves shells (calcium carbonate).
Sea level rise causes:
(1) thermal expansion of water, (2) melting glaciers/ice sheets.
IPCC:
Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change — synthesizes climate science. Reports used for policy.

Step 1:

FRQ Calculation: Energy Flow
1
Given:
A producer trophic level contains 100,000 kcal.
2
Primary consumers receive:
100,000 × 0.10 = 10,000 kcal
3
Secondary consumers receive:
10,000 × 0.10 = 1,000 kcal
4
Tertiary consumers receive:
1,000 × 0.10 = 100 kcal
5
Units matter:
Always include units (kcal, kg, %). Show each multiplication step.

Oceans absorb ~30% of CO₂ emissions. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ → lowers pH. Since industrialization, ocean pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1 — a 25% increase in acidity (pH is logarithmic). This threatens shell-forming organisms (oysters, corals, pteropods).
Perfect Score Checklist
Rule of 70: doubling time = 70 ÷ growth rate (%)
Growth rate = (birth rate − death rate) / 10
(per 1000 → percentage)
10% energy rule: each trophic level has ~10% of the energy of the one below
HIPPCO: Habitat, Invasive, Population, Pollution, Climate, Overexploitation
Montreal Protocol (1987) = CFCs/ozone. Paris Agreement (2015) = climate change
Eutrophication: nutrients → algae bloom → O₂ depletion → dead zone
Primary pollutant = directly emitted. Secondary = formed in atmosphere (ozone, smog, acid rain)
DTM Stage 2: high birth, falling death rates = fastest population growth
Ocean acidification: CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → threatens calcium carbonate shells
APES FRQ always has a calculation — show formula, substitution, and units for full credit
Point source pollution = identifiable single source (regulated). Nonpoint = diffuse (hard to regulate).

<h3 class="sub"><span class="sub-icon">🌿</span>Population Ecology — Key Equations</h3><div class="code-block"><span class="hl">Rule of 70 (doubling time):</span>
Doubling time (years) = 70 / annual growth rate (%)
Example: 2% growth rate → 70/2 = 35 years to double

<span class="hl">Population growth rate:</span>
% growth rate = [(birth rate − death rate) / 1000] × 100
(or add immigration / subtract emigration for total change)

<span class="hl">Total Fertility Rate (TFR):</span>
TFR &lt; 2.1 → population eventually DECREASES (below replacement)
TFR = 2.1 → stable population
TFR &gt; 2.1 → population GROWS

<script>function pick(el, isCorrect, qId) {
  const qItem = el.closest('.q-item');
  if (qItem.classList.contains('answered')) return;
  qItem.classList.add('answered');
  el.classList.add(isCorrect ? 'correct' : 'wrong');
  if (!isCorrect) {
    qItem.querySelectorAll('.q-choice[data-correct="true"]').forEach(c => c.classList.add('show-correct'));
  }
  qItem.querySelectorAll('.q-choice').forEach(c => c.classList.add('picked'));
  const explain = document.getElementById('exp-' + qId);
  if (explain) explain.classList.add('show');
  // update score
  const quiz = el.closest('.quiz-section');
  const answered = quiz.querySelectorAll('.q-item.answered').length;
  const correct = quiz.querySelectorAll('.q-choice.correct').length;
  const total = quiz.querySelectorAll('.q-item').length;
  const scoreEl = quiz.querySelector('.quiz-score');
  if (scoreEl) scoreEl.textContent = correct + '/' + answered + ' correct (' + total + ' total)';
}
function resetQuiz(btn) {
  const quiz = btn.closest('.quiz-section');
  quiz.querySelectorAll('.q-item').forEach(q => {
    q.classList.remove('answered');
    q.querySelectorAll('.q-choice').forEach(c => c.classList.remove('correct','wrong','show-correct','picked'));
    q.querySelectorAll('.q-explain').forEach(e => e.classList.remove('show'));
  });
  const scoreEl = quiz.querySelector('.quiz-score');
  if (scoreEl) scoreEl.textContent = '0/0 correct';
}
